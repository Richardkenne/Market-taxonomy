Market, Business & Delivery Models Taxonomy

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about the three-part taxonomy framework — Markets, Business Systems, and Delivery Models.

What is the Market Taxonomy?

The Market Taxonomy is a structured, three-level classification of 43 globally recognised macro markets — the same markets used by institutional investors, regulators, and major consultancies. Each market is broken down into Level 1 segments, Level 2 niches, and Level 3 specific problems.

It answers the question: where does your business operate?

Why do most markets have 5–6 Level 1 categories?

The number of Level 1 categories is not predefined. Across most macro markets, convergent validation by institutional authorities, market intelligence, operators, and capital allocation actors typically results in a limited set of stable categories. This recurring pattern reflects structural clarity and institutional practice, not an imposed numerical constraint.

What is a Business System?

A Business System is a revenue-generation mechanism — how money enters the firm. It is independent of market, technology, or era, and expressible in accounting terms.

There are exactly 12 Business Systems. Any business in the world operates through one or more of them. If a proposed "business model" cannot be mapped to one of these 12, it is a market label, a technology, or a delivery form — not a business model.

What are Unit Economics, and why are they inside each Business System card?

Unit Economics are the core financial metrics that determine whether a Business System is structurally viable. They are system-specific — once the monetisation mechanism is fixed, the same constraints appear across different markets, geographies, and technologies.

Each of the 12 cards includes a collapsible Unit Economics section showing: the relevant unit of measurement, key thresholds (in red), and a structural viability rule.

These benchmarks are viability constraints, not growth targets. Falling outside them requires a clear structural explanation.

What is the difference between a Business System and a business model?

A Business System is the primitive — the structural mechanism through which revenue is generated. A "business model" as commonly used is often a combination of market label, delivery form, and business system conflated together.

Example: "We are a SaaS company" describes a Delivery Model, not a Business System. The Business System is Subscription (if recurring) or Usage-Based (if metered). SaaS is how value is delivered, not how money enters the firm.

What is a Delivery Model?

A Delivery Model defines how value reaches the user — how the customer accesses it, how the provider operates it, and how it is consumed or experienced.

There are 12 universal Delivery Models, covering digital (SaaS, API-First, On-Premise, Open-Core), physical (Direct/D2C, Retail, On-demand, Wholesale), and service (In-person, Remote, Platform/Ecosystem, Hybrid).

→ Why Delivery Models are universal and finite

Are Business Systems and Delivery Models the same thing?

No — they are orthogonal axes and must never be conflated.

Business System = how money enters the firm (e.g. Subscription).
Delivery Model = how value reaches the user (e.g. SaaS).

The same Business System can pair with multiple Delivery Models. A law firm (Service / Retainer) can deliver in-person or remotely — same system, different delivery. A subscription business can be delivered as SaaS, desktop software, or a physical box.

Do Delivery Models apply to physical businesses too?

Yes — the 12 Delivery Models are universal. They cover a Christmas ornament manufacturer (Direct Physical / Wholesale), a construction contractor (In-person / On-site), a hair brand (Retail / D2C), a Bitcoin fund (Asset Ownership as Business System; no specific delivery model required beyond holding), and a law firm specialising in IPOs (In-person + Remote).

New technologies change implementation, not structure. E-commerce modernised Direct Physical; SaaS modernised software delivery. The categories remain stable.

How do the three parts fit together?

The three parts answer three distinct questions:

Part A — Market: where does your business operate? (e.g. Healthcare, Real Estate, Software)

Part B — Business System: how does money enter your firm? (e.g. Subscription, Transaction Fee, Service / Retainer)

Part C — Delivery Model: how does value reach your user? (e.g. SaaS, In-person, Direct Physical)

A complete business description requires all three. "We are a SaaS healthcare company" defines Market (Healthcare) and Delivery Model (SaaS) — but not the Business System. That description is incomplete until the revenue mechanism is specified.
